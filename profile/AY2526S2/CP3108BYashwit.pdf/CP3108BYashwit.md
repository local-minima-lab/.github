Gradient Descent is perhaps the most prominent optimization method in the Machine Learning domain, especially with the growing dominance of Deep Neural Networks. From the simple Steepest Descent algorithm that is taught in undergraduate courses to the cutting-edge Adam optimizer used in many industry contexts, Gradient Descent has only evolved but is never absent. Still, little and less is known by most about the exact motivations behind both the initial popularity and continued relevance of Gradient Descent within increasingly sophisticated ML optimization algorithms.

The purpose of this report is therefore to survey, both analytically and empirically, the development of the Gradient Descent method from its inauguration in 1847 as a novel method of solving nonlinear systems to its deployment in now highly sophisticated and yet difficult-to-interpret Deep Learning optimizers.

In general, this report is interested in the geometric convergence of Gradient Descent - and its many forms - in terms of the proximity of its iterates to the optimal solution, the rate of convergence itself, and the zeroing of the gradient magnitude.
